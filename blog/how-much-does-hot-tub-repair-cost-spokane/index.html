How Much Does Hot Tub Repair Cost in Spokane?

A complete 2026 pricing guide for Spokane-area hot tub owners.

March 2026 5 min read

If your hot tub is acting up, one of the first questions on your mind is probably: how much is this going to cost? The answer depends on the type of repair, the parts involved, and the age of your spa. Here is a straightforward breakdown of what Spokane homeowners can expect to pay in 2026.

Common Hot Tub Repair Costs in Spokane

Most hot tub repairs in Spokane fall between $150 and $1,500. Below are the most common repairs we see and their typical price ranges, including parts and labor.

Heating Element Replacement — $200–$400

A failed heating element is one of the most common reasons hot tubs stop heating. The element itself typically costs $50–$150, with the remainder going to diagnostic time and labor. If your water is lukewarm or stone cold, a burned-out heating element is often the culprit. In Spokane's cold winters, a functioning heater is not optional — it is essential.

Pump Repair or Replacement — $300–$600

Hot tub pumps handle water circulation and power the jets. When a pump fails, you will notice weak jets, unusual humming or grinding sounds, or no water movement at all. Minor pump repairs (like a seal replacement) fall on the lower end. A full pump replacement for a two-speed jet pump will be closer to $500–$600. Circulation pumps are typically less expensive.

Control Board Replacement — $400–$800

The control board is the brain of your hot tub. When it fails, you may see error codes on the topside panel, erratic behavior, or a completely unresponsive spa. Board replacements are on the higher end because the parts themselves are expensive and must match your specific hot tub model. We always try to repair the existing board first when possible to save you money.

Jet Replacement — $150–$300

Cracked, stuck, or leaking jets are a common issue, especially in older hot tubs. Individual jet bodies are inexpensive ($10–$30 each), but the labor to access and replace internal jet assemblies can add up if multiple jets need attention. Surface-level jet face replacements are quick and affordable.

Leak Repair — $200–$500

Leaks can originate from jet connections, pump unions, the heater manifold, or cracked PVC plumbing. The cost depends heavily on where the leak is located and how accessible it is. A visible leak at a pump union might be a $200 fix. A leak buried deep inside the cabinet behind the foam insulation will take longer to locate and repair, pushing the cost toward $400–$500.

Factors That Affect Your Repair Cost

Several variables can move your final bill up or down:

  • Age of the hot tub — Older spas may need hard-to-find parts or have multiple issues compounding the problem.
  • Brand and model — Premium brands sometimes require proprietary parts that cost more. Common brands like Hot Spring or Jacuzzi have widely available parts.
  • Extent of the damage — A single failed component is straightforward. If corrosion or water damage has affected multiple systems, the repair scope grows.
  • Accessibility — Components that are easy to reach cost less to repair. Repairs requiring full cabinet removal or foam excavation take more labor hours.
  • Urgency — Standard scheduling (24–48 hours) is our normal rate. Emergency same-day service may carry an additional fee depending on availability.

When to Repair vs. Replace Your Hot Tub

As a general rule, if the repair costs more than 50% of what a comparable new hot tub would cost, replacement starts to make more sense. Here are some other factors to consider:

  • Repair if the tub is under 10 years old, the issue is isolated to one component, and the shell and frame are in good shape.
  • Replace if the tub is 15+ years old, you are facing multiple simultaneous failures, or the shell is cracked or delaminating.
  • Ask us — we will always give you an honest assessment. We do not push unnecessary repairs, and we will tell you if replacement is the smarter investment.
